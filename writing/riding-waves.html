Lessons from Riding Waves, Applied to Life

April 2026


I’m a beginner at surfing. Even though I’m still learning, I can’t swim at all, and I’ve almost drowned once while trying as an adult.

The last time I was in water deeper than I could stand in was back in grade 1, during a school swimming session. Another kid pushed me into the deep end while no one was looking, and I panicked and barely managed to grab the edge of the pool and pull myself up.

The only thing standing between me and disaster has been my surfboard and its leash.

But to stop taking dumb risks, I’m also learning to swim, slowly, step by step.

Surfing has been this incredible thing I’ve discovered that puts me into a flow state.

In those moments, my mind focuses only on paddling toward the right wave, trying to stand up, balancing as I ride, and most importantly, not drowning.

Through all of this, I’ve picked up a few principles from surfing that surprisingly also apply to life and even startups.

You don’t need the fanciest or coolest gear to start. Surfing began with people balancing on simple wooden boards. Use what you already have and begin from there.

You don’t need to stand up and do tricks right away. Mastering the basics, balance, paddling, and staying steady, is what matters most at the start.

You can’t wait for the perfect wave. You just have to go with whatever comes your way, and if it doesn’t take you far enough, try again.

Don’t wait for the perfect moment. You might think you should hold back until the perfect wave arrives, but even if it does, you might still miss it. Start with what’s in front of you.

Someone splashing around, laughing, trying things out, and testing the waves often learns faster than the one paddling with clenched teeth.

When it’s your first time, having a coach or mentor makes a big difference. Someone with more experience can help you see mistakes you wouldn’t notice on your own.

You can watch endless videos, movies, or reels about surfing, but you’ll never learn until you get in the water yourself. Action matters more than theory.

You’ll see people riding massive waves, pulling off tricks, and looking effortless. It’s easy to compare yourself and feel small, but you’re on your own path. Focus on your own progress and tune out the noise.

When a wave knocks you down, it’s not the end. You always have the chance to climb back on your board and try again.

When you’re out at sea, that’s when you really understand what you’re good at or where you need work, whether it’s balancing on the board, changing direction, or swimming through the waves to get to the right spot.

You can also learn a lot by watching others. Notice how they move, the choices they make, and why. Then try it yourself until you figure out what works for you.

You can’t really see how far you’ve come from the beach. You only notice when you look back.

Don’t rush into the deepest water or the biggest waves when you’re just starting out. Begin small, build confidence, and grow step by step.

Some days you’ll give it your best and still feel unhappy with how it went. That’s okay. Tomorrow is a new day, with new waves waiting.
